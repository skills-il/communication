# Israeli Anti-Spam Law (Chok HaSpam)

## Overview

Amendment 40 to the Communications Law (Telecommunications), 5742-1982, commonly known as "Chok HaSpam" (the Spam Law), is Israel's primary legislation governing unsolicited commercial communications. It applies to email, SMS, fax, and automated phone calls.

## Key Provisions

### Prior Explicit Consent (Section 30A)

Unlike the US CAN-SPAM Act which uses an opt-out model, Israeli law requires **prior explicit consent** ("haskama mukdemet") before sending any commercial message. The consent must be:

- **Explicit**: The recipient must actively agree (pre-checked boxes are not valid)
- **Documented**: The sender must retain proof of consent (timestamp, method, source)
- **Specific**: Consent should specify the communication channel (email, SMS, etc.)
- **Revocable**: The recipient can withdraw consent at any time

### Exceptions to Prior Consent

Consent is not required when ALL of the following conditions are met:

1. The recipient is an **existing customer** who provided their contact details during a purchase
2. The message relates to **similar products or services** to those previously purchased
3. The customer was given a **clear opportunity to refuse** at the point of data collection
4. No more than **12 months** have passed since the last transaction

### Unsubscribe Requirements

Every commercial message must include:

- A clear, prominent unsubscribe mechanism in Hebrew
- The sender's identity and contact information
- Processing within **2 business days** (not 10 as in CAN-SPAM)
- No requirement for the recipient to log in or provide additional information

### Penalties

- **Civil liability**: Up to 1,000 NIS per message sent without consent, without requiring proof of damages
- **Class action lawsuits**: Common in Israel for spam violations
- **Criminal penalties**: Fines for repeat or large-scale violations
- **Regulatory enforcement**: The Israel Internet Association and courts handle complaints

## Comparison with International Laws

| Aspect | Israel (Chok HaSpam) | US (CAN-SPAM) | EU (GDPR + ePrivacy) |
|--------|----------------------|----------------|------------------------|
| Model | Opt-in | Opt-out | Opt-in |
| Consent | Prior explicit | Not required | Prior explicit |
| Unsubscribe | 2 business days | 10 business days | Without delay |
| Damages | 1,000 NIS/msg (no proof needed) | Up to $46,517/email | Up to 4% revenue |
| B2B exception | Limited | Broad | Varies by country |

## Israeli-Specific Considerations

### Robinson List (Do Not Disturb Registry)

Israel maintains a national "Do Not Disturb" registry. Senders should check recipient numbers against this list before sending commercial messages. This applies primarily to phone calls and SMS but establishes a consumer expectation of consent-based communication.

### Shabbat and Holidays

While not legally mandated for email, sending commercial emails during Shabbat (Friday evening to Saturday evening) and Jewish holidays is considered poor practice and may increase unsubscribe rates in the Israeli market.

### Hebrew Language Requirements

No specific legal requirement for Hebrew language, but:
- Unsubscribe mechanisms should be in Hebrew for Israeli recipients
- Consumer Protection Law disclosures must be comprehensible to the recipient
- Hebrew RTL formatting is expected by Israeli ISPs and improves deliverability

## Enforcement Trends

Israeli courts have been increasingly active in enforcing Chok HaSpam:
- Class action suits against spammers are common and often successful
- Courts have awarded damages even for small numbers of unsolicited messages
- The burden of proof for consent lies with the sender

## Practical Compliance Checklist

1. Obtain and document explicit consent before sending
2. Maintain a consent database with timestamps, methods, and sources
3. Include Hebrew unsubscribe mechanism in every commercial email
4. Process unsubscribe requests within 2 business days
5. Include sender identity and contact information
6. Check against Do Not Disturb registry (for SMS/phone)
7. Retain consent records for at least 3 years after last communication
8. Implement separate consent tracking per communication channel
