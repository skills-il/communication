# Equal Employment Opportunities Law 1988 — CV Compliance Checklist

The Employment (Equal Opportunities) Law 5748-1988 prohibits Israeli employers from discriminating in hiring based on specific protected characteristics. Including these on a CV creates legal risk for both the employer and the candidate (signal of willingness to be evaluated on prohibited grounds).

## Protected Characteristics (Do Not Include)

| Characteristic | Legal Basis | CV Action |
|----------------|-------------|-----------|
| Age | Section 2(a) | Omit date of birth, graduation year for older candidates |
| Gender | Section 2(a) | Omit gender identifier, use neutral pronouns where possible |
| Sexual orientation | Section 2(a), 1992 amendment | Omit any reference |
| Personal / marital status | Section 2(a) | Omit marital status |
| Pregnancy | Section 2(a) | Omit pregnancy or family planning info |
| Fertility treatments | Section 2(a), 2007 amendment | Omit |
| Parenthood | Section 2(a) | Omit number of children, parental leave status |
| Race | Section 2(a) | Omit race / ethnicity |
| Religion | Section 2(a) | Omit religious affiliation (exception: religious role at religious institution) |
| Nationality / origin | Section 2(a) | Omit country of origin (exception: when work permit status is job-relevant) |
| Country of origin | Section 2(a) | Omit |
| Political views | Section 2(a) | Omit political affiliation, activism |
| Place of residence | Section 2(a) | City only is fine for commute signaling. No street address, no neighborhood that could signal demographic |
| Reserve military service | Section 2(a) | Omit frequency or specific reserve deployment schedule |

## Practical Checklist Before Submitting a CV

Run through this list before sending any Israeli CV:

- [ ] No photograph on the English version
- [ ] No date of birth anywhere
- [ ] No marital status or family status
- [ ] No number of children
- [ ] No religious affiliation (unless the role is at a religious institution)
- [ ] No political affiliation or activism
- [ ] No ID number (Teudat Zehut)
- [ ] No home address — city only
- [ ] No "expected salary" line (salary is negotiated, not pre-committed)
- [ ] No reference list ("References available upon request" is also noise — remove it)
- [ ] Graduation year is acceptable if you are under ~35; older candidates may omit year and list only degree + institution

## When Photos Are Allowed

Photos are NEVER required in Israel and should always be omitted from the English CV. For Hebrew CVs, there are narrow exceptions:

| Role type | Photo allowed? |
|-----------|----------------|
| Tech, engineering, finance, legal, medicine, academia | No |
| Customer-facing corporate (receptionist, sales) | Discouraged but common |
| Acting, modeling, hospitality, flight attendant | Yes — it is a bona fide occupational qualification |

Even where "allowed", the photo invites unconscious bias. If the user does not explicitly request it, leave it out.

## Bona Fide Occupational Qualification (BFOQ)

Section 2(c) of the law permits employers to consider a protected characteristic only when it is an "essential requirement" for the role. Example: a dance academy hiring a ballerina can consider physical ability; a church hiring a pastor can consider religion. These exceptions are narrow and almost never apply to tech, office, or service roles.

If a candidate is applying to a BFOQ role (e.g., acting), the normally-prohibited fields (photo, age, gender) become acceptable. The skill should still only include them if the user explicitly asks.

## What the Employer Should Also Not Ask

This is for the candidate's awareness during interviews. If an interviewer asks any of these, the candidate can legally decline to answer:

- "How old are you?"
- "Are you married?"
- "Do you plan to have children?"
- "What religion are you?"
- "Where are your parents from?"
- "Are you pregnant?"
- "What is your sexual orientation?"
- "What are your political views?"
- "When were you in reserves last?"

## Enforcement

The Equal Employment Opportunities Commission (נציבות שוויון הזדמנויות בעבודה) enforces the law. Candidates who experience discrimination can file a complaint with the commission, which can result in fines for the employer and damages for the candidate.
