## Benefits and Total Compensation

### Mandatory Benefits (Required by Israeli Law)

| Benefit | Rate | Notes |
|---------|------|-------|
| Pension (employer) | 6.5% of salary | Mandatory from day one |
| Pension (employee) | 6% of salary | Deducted from gross |
| Severance (Section 14) | 8.33% of salary | Deposited to pension fund monthly |
| Vacation | 12-20 days/year | By seniority (12 for years 1-5, 14 for year 6, 15 for year 7, +1/year up to 20) |
| Sick days | 1.5 days/month | Up to 90 days accumulated |
| Convalescence pay | 5-10 days/year (by seniority) | ~418 NIS/day (2024 rate, subject to adjustment) |

### Total Compensation Calculator

To compare offers, calculate total annual compensation:

```
Base salary (monthly gross x 12)
+ Annual bonus (if guaranteed or expected)
+ Keren Hishtalmut employer contribution (salary x 7.5% x 12)
+ Pension employer contribution (salary x 6.5% x 12)
+ Severance contribution (salary x 8.33% x 12)
+ Education budget (annual)
+ Meal benefit (monthly value x 12)
+ Car/phone allowance (monthly x 12)
+ Estimated equity value (if applicable)
= Total Annual Compensation
```

**Example: Senior Backend Developer, 45,000 NIS/month:**
```
Base salary:          540,000 NIS/year
Bonus (1.5 months):   67,500 NIS/year
Keren Hishtalmut:      40,500 NIS/year (7.5% employer)
Pension:               35,100 NIS/year (6.5% employer)
Severance:             44,982 NIS/year (8.33%)
Education budget:       8,000 NIS/year
Meals (1,000/mo):      12,000 NIS/year
Convalescence:          3,762 NIS/year (9 days x 418)
                     ---------
Total:               ~751,844 NIS/year
                     (~62,654 NIS/month effective)
```

The total employer cost is approximately 39% higher than the base salary in this example.

## Equity Compensation Guide

### 102 ESOP Tax Tracks

Israel offers two main tax tracks for employee stock options:

**102 Capital Gains Track (Trustee) (most common):**
- Options held by a licensed trustee for minimum 24 months
- Gains taxed at 25% capital gains rate
- Employee pays tax only upon sale (not exercise)
- Company does NOT get a tax deduction (trade-off for the employee's lower tax rate)
- Most preferred by employees

**102 Ordinary Income Track (Trustee):**
- Options held by trustee for minimum 12 months
- Gains taxed as ordinary income (up to 50%)
- Company gets a larger tax deduction
- Less favorable for employees
- Used when company wants larger deduction

**3(i) Track (Non-trustee):**
- No trustee requirement
- Taxed as ordinary income upon exercise
- Full tax burden on the employee
- Used for short-term grants or contractors
- Less common in Israeli tech

### Standard Vesting Terms

- **4-year vesting with 1-year cliff**: Industry standard
- **Monthly vesting after cliff**: Most common (some do quarterly)
- **Exercise window**: 90 days after departure (standard), 1-3 years (generous)
- **Acceleration**: Single trigger (exercise on acquisition) or double trigger (exercise on acquisition + termination)

## Negotiation Reference Points

### What to Negotiate (in order of typical impact)

1. **Base salary**: Direct financial impact, affects all percentage-based benefits
2. **Equity**: Highest potential upside, especially at early-stage companies
3. **Keren Hishtalmut**: Tax-advantaged, essentially 7.5% salary bonus
4. **Vacation days**: Quality of life, standard to negotiate 20-25 days
5. **Signing bonus**: One-time payment, useful for bridging salary gaps
6. **Work flexibility**: WFH days, flexible hours
7. **Education budget**: Professional development investment
8. **Title**: Can affect future earning potential

### Salary Negotiation Data Points

When negotiating, reference these data points:
- "Based on Ethosia salary survey data for my role and experience level..."
- "LinkedIn Salary Insights shows the median for this role in Tel Aviv is..."
- "I have a competing offer at [range] for a similar role"
- "My current total compensation including keren hishtalmut and bonuses is..."

### Common Negotiation Mistakes in Israel

1. **Not negotiating at all**: Israeli employers expect negotiation
2. **Focusing only on base salary**: Total compensation matters more
3. **Ignoring keren hishtalmut**: This is a significant tax-advantaged benefit
4. **Not understanding equity**: Know the company's valuation, your percentage, and the tax track
5. **Comparing gross to gross without benefits**: Two offers at the same gross salary can have very different total compensation
6. **Delaying too long**: The Israeli market moves fast. Respond to offers within 3-5 business days

## Regional Salary Context

### Tel Aviv Metro (Gush Dan)

The heart of Israeli tech. Highest salaries, most competitive market.
- Average tech salary: ~40,000 NIS/month (2026)
- Cost of living: Highest in Israel
- Rent (2BR): 7,000-12,000 NIS/month
- Commute: Often stressful, WFH policies common

### Herzliya / Ra'anana / Kfar Saba

The "Silicon Wadi" corridor north of Tel Aviv. Many tech campuses and R&D centers.
- Salaries comparable to Tel Aviv
- Lower cost of living than Tel Aviv proper
- Many multinational R&D centers (Microsoft, Oracle, HP, SAP)
- Growing startup scene

### Haifa

Israel's northern tech hub, anchored by the Technion and Haifa University.
- Salaries 5-10% lower than Tel Aviv
- Significantly lower cost of living
- Strong in hardware, embedded systems, autonomous vehicles
- Intel, Elbit, and many defense companies

### Be'er Sheva

Growing tech ecosystem around Ben-Gurion University and CyberSpark.
- Salaries 10-15% lower than Tel Aviv
- Much lower cost of living
- Strong in cybersecurity (IDF Cyber campus nearby)
- Government incentives for tech companies

### Jerusalem

Smaller tech scene but growing.
- Salaries 5-10% lower than Tel Aviv
- Moderate cost of living
- Strong in biotech, health-tech, and enterprise software
- Mobileye headquarters
